A Short AI Decision Guide for Founders & Directors

Thinking support for the decisions you're already making

You don't need another tutorial on prompts. You need a way to think clearly about where AI fits in your organisation—and where it doesn't.

This guide offers a simple framework for deciding which tasks to give to AI, which to supervise closely, and which to keep entirely human. It's designed to be read once and referred to when needed.

The core question

When considering any task, ask: What is the cost of getting this wrong?

Not "Can AI do this?"—it probably can. The better question is whether AI should do this, given what's at stake.

This leads to three categories of work.

Green tasks: low risk, high repeatability

These are the tasks that drain time without requiring your judgement. They follow predictable patterns. They rarely carry reputational or commercial consequences if done imperfectly.

  • Summarising meeting notes into actions
  • Drafting a first-pass internal document
  • Turning rough notes into a structured agenda
  • Producing a first draft of an email you will still edit

A simple test: if you can describe what "good" looks like in a few bullet points, AI can help.

For green tasks, the goal is speed. Let AI produce the first draft. Review briefly. Move on.

Amber tasks: helpful, but only with a guardrail

These tasks are worth accelerating, but the cost of a mistake is real. A factual error could undermine credibility. A wrong tone could damage a relationship. A vague scope could cost you money.

  • Client-facing content where accuracy matters
  • Proposals and scope documents
  • Marketing copy that needs to sound like you
  • Research summaries that will inform decisions

For amber tasks, the aim isn't perfection on the first pass. It's a repeatable process that catches errors before they leave your desk.

Layer and Loop

  1. Layer — Add your judgement over the draft. Check facts. Adjust tone. Remove anything you wouldn't say yourself.
  2. Loop — Run through it once more to tighten accuracy and consistency.
  3. Stop — Then you stop. Not endlessly. Just enough to keep quality where it needs to be.

This is where most senior professionals will spend their time with AI. Not automating, but accelerating—with appropriate oversight.

Red tasks: keep human ownership

These are the areas where trust is the product. Where your judgement is exactly what clients, boards, or regulators are paying for. Where being quoted matters.

  • Pricing and commercial positioning
  • Sensitive client conversations
  • Anything that could be quoted back publicly
  • Legal or regulatory commitments (always take proper advice)
  • Communications during a crisis

AI can support your thinking here—helping you structure options, stress-test logic, or draft starting points. But it shouldn't be driving.

The responsibility remains with you. The final words should be yours. The decision certainly should be.

A note on judgement

The boundaries between green, amber, and red will vary by organisation, by sector, by moment. A draft email might be green in one context and amber in another. A piece of analysis might shift from amber to red when the stakes rise.

This framework isn't a rulebook. It's a way of thinking—one that puts responsibility at the centre and keeps the technology in service of your judgement, not the other way around.

The question worth asking regularly

As AI becomes more capable, the temptation will be to hand over more. That's not necessarily wrong. But it's worth pausing periodically to ask:

Are we using AI to do things faster, or to avoid thinking about them?

The best use of these tools is the former. The risk is the latter.

Your role as a founder or director is to know the difference.
